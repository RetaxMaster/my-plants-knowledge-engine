# Boston Fern (*Nephrolepis exaltata*)

## Overview
The Boston fern, *Nephrolepis exaltata*, is one of the most beloved and enduring houseplants in the world. Recognizable by its graceful, arching fronds covered in many small "ladder-rung" leaflets (pinnae), it has been a fixture of porches, parlors, and bright bathrooms since the Victorian era. The cultivar most people grow indoors is 'Bostoniensis', which gave the plant its common name. It is also widely known as the **sword fern**.

## Native Habitat
This fern has a broad, essentially pantropical distribution. It is native to humid tropical and subtropical forests of Florida, the West Indies, Mexico, Central and South America, Polynesia, and parts of Africa. In the wild it grows as an understory and sometimes epiphytic plant, thriving in warm, shaded, consistently moist, and very humid conditions beneath the forest canopy. Understanding this origin is the key to keeping it happy at home: it wants warmth, steady moisture, filtered light, and humidity. Outdoors it is hardy only in mild, frost-free climates (roughly USDA zones 9–11, with some references extending to 8B–12).

## Light
Boston ferns do best in **bright, indirect light** — for example, an east-facing window, or set back from a south- or west-facing window behind a sheer curtain. They tolerate medium and somewhat lower light, but direct, harsh sun will scorch and bleach the delicate fronds. Outdoors they belong in partial to full shade.

## Water & Soil
Consistency is everything. Keep the potting medium **evenly and consistently moist but never waterlogged** — these ferns are unforgiving of drying out and will quickly shed fronds if the soil goes dry, yet their fronds can also rot if kept saturated. Use a peaty, well-drained but moisture-retentive potting mix (peat or coir with vermiculite works well). Among commonly grown ferns it is one of the more drought-tolerant once established, but it is best treated as a moisture lover. Reduce watering modestly from autumn through late winter when growth slows.

## Temperature
Aim for ordinary warm room temperatures of about **18–24 °C (64–75 °F)**. It can tolerate brief dips toward the low single digits Celsius and warm spells up to the mid-30s °C, but it is not frost-hardy and should be brought indoors well before temperatures approach freezing. Avoid cold drafts and hot, dry air from heating vents.

## Humidity
This is the make-or-break factor. Boston ferns **crave high humidity** and resent dry indoor air, which causes the classic browning and crisping of frond tips and edges. Aim for at least 50% relative humidity, ideally 70% or higher. Boost humidity by grouping plants, setting the pot on a tray of wet pebbles, double-potting with damp sphagnum moss, or using a humidifier. Bright bathrooms and kitchens are natural homes for them.

## Fertilizing
Feed lightly during the active growing season (spring through early autumn) with a **balanced liquid houseplant fertilizer at half strength, roughly every 4–6 weeks (about monthly)**. Ferns are sensitive to over-feeding — excess fertilizer salts cause frond browning — so err on the weak side. Reduce or stop feeding in winter.

## Repotting & Division
Repot or divide every couple of years, ideally in spring. Signs it is time include roots filling the pot or escaping the drainage holes, soil drying out unusually fast, and a crowded crown with weaker growth. To divide, cut the root ball vertically, keeping as many fronds as possible per division — this is also the easiest way to propagate the plant.

## Maintenance & Common Pests
Maintenance is low. Remove old, dead, or browning fronds at the base to keep the plant tidy and encourage fresh growth; if a plant has been stressed and shed heavily, it can be cut back hard (to about 5 cm / 2 inches) and will regenerate from the roots. Rotate the plant periodically for even growth. Watch for **scale insects, mealybugs, spider mites, whitefly**, and, when grown outdoors, **slugs and snails**. Occasional fungal problems and root rot can occur in overly wet conditions.

## Fun Facts
- The Boston fern became wildly popular after a sport (natural mutation) with gracefully arching fronds was discovered among a shipment of ferns sent to Boston in 1894 — hence the name.
- It is frequently cited in lists of houseplants studied for their air-purifying and humidifying qualities, and is non-toxic to cats and dogs, making it a pet-safe choice.
