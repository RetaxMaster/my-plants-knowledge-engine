# Dracaena fragrans — Corn Plant

## Overview
*Dracaena fragrans*, known as the **corn plant** or **cornstalk dracaena**, is one of the most popular and forgiving indoor foliage plants in the world. Its glossy, arching, strap-shaped leaves grow from a thick woody cane, giving it the look of a young corn stalk — hence the common name. Indoors it usually stays a manageable 4–6 feet (1.5–2.5 m) tall, though in the wild it can soar to 15 feet (4.5 m) or more. The popular variegated form 'Massangeana' carries a broad yellow-green stripe down the center of each leaf.

## Native Habitat
The species is native to **tropical Africa**, where it lives as an understory plant in warm, humid, frost-free forests and woodlands. There it receives dappled, filtered light beneath the canopy rather than harsh direct sun — which is exactly why it adapts so well to the moderate light of a typical home or office. Its tropical origin also explains its dislike of cold drafts and temperatures below about 50°F (10°C).

## Light
Corn plant thrives in **bright, indirect light** and should be protected from prolonged direct sun, which scorches the foliage and bleaches the variegation. It is notably tolerant of **lower light** — it can survive several feet from a window — but in deep shade the leaves narrow and growth slows. Aim for a bright spot out of direct rays for the best color and fullest growth.

## Water
Keep the soil **uniformly moist during the growing season**, letting the **top inch or so dry out between waterings**, but never allowing the rootball to dry out completely. From fall through late winter, **reduce watering** as growth slows. Always water thoroughly until it drains from the bottom, and discard any water in the saucer — soggy, poorly drained soil leads to root rot, the most common cause of decline. A useful note: dracaenas are **very sensitive to fluoride** (and to chlorine), so brown leaf tips and margins often come from tap water; using filtered water or rainwater helps avoid this.

## Temperature
The corn plant grows best between **60–75°F (15–24°C)** and prefers steady conditions without sudden swings. Keep it away from cold drafts, heating vents, and air conditioners. Do not let it experience temperatures below **50°F (10°C)**; sustained cold causes leaf damage and stunting. If summered outdoors, bring it back inside before nights cool down in early fall.

## Humidity
This is a tropical plant that appreciates **moderate to higher humidity**, ideally around **40–50%**. When household humidity drops below **30–40%** — common in winter with indoor heating — the plant benefits from occasional misting, a pebble tray, or a humidifier. Dry leaf tips and edges are a classic sign of air that is too dry.

## Fertilizing
Feed lightly with a **balanced liquid houseplant fertilizer about once a month during spring and summer** (the active growing season). **Reduce or stop feeding in fall and winter** when growth naturally slows. Avoid fertilizers containing superphosphate, which can worsen the plant's fluoride sensitivity.

## Repotting
Corn plant is slow-growing and happy being somewhat pot-bound, so it only needs repotting roughly **every two years**. Signs it's time: roots emerging from the drainage holes, roots circling inside the pot, water running straight through, or noticeably slowed growth. The RHS suggests simply top-dressing or potting on in spring using a loam-based, well-draining mix.

## Maintenance & Common Pests
No routine pruning is needed. If the cane becomes too tall, leggy, or bare at the base, you can **cut it back to the desired height in spring** — new leaf clusters will sprout just below the cut, and the removed sections can be rooted as new plants. Wipe the broad leaves occasionally to remove dust and keep them photosynthesizing well; rotating the plant periodically keeps growth even.

Watch for **spider mites, thrips, scale, and mealybugs**, especially in dry indoor air. The plant is also prone to **leaf-spot diseases and root rot** when overwatered or poorly drained.

## Toxicity
**Dracaena fragrans is toxic to cats and dogs** (and mildly to humans) if ingested. It contains saponins, which can cause vomiting, drooling, loss of appetite, and depression in pets. Keep it out of reach of curious animals and children.

## Fun Fact
Despite the name *fragrans* ("fragrant"), the corn plant rarely blooms indoors — but when it does (usually only on large, mature, well-established specimens), it produces clusters of small white flowers with an intensely sweet, almost overpowering nighttime perfume.
